That makes perfect sense and is actually a classic "Inside-Out" enterprise strategy. Starting with an internal employee tool is the safest way to "battle-test" your logic before letting the general public touch it.
By building for employees first, you get a "controlled environment" where users are more forgiving of small bugs, and you can refine the "Agnostic Engine" based on the actual messy files they deal with every day.
1. The "Inside-Out" Roadmap
We will structure the plan as a two-phase rollout. This is a core part of the "Completion-Proof" strategy because it gives you a major win (Phase 1) before you take on the complexity of a public-facing site (Phase 2).
Phase 1: The Internal Utility (The "Employee Optimizer")
Focus: Speed and power.
Deployment: Standalone internal site or browser extension.
Goal: Allow employees to take "bad" files they’ve already received and "fix" them instantly.
Value: Saves the company money on Adobe licenses and reduces "cleanup" time.
Phase 2: The External Gateway (The "Client Intake")
Focus: Simplicity and "Grandma-proof" UI.
Deployment: Integrated directly into the company’s public upload page.
Goal: Prevent bad files from ever reaching the employees.
Value: Improves the customer experience (CX) and automates the entire triage process.
2. Updated Manual Sections: Transition Planning
To reflect this, we need to add a "Transition Strategy" section to your Internal Manual.
Internal Manual: Business Strategy
The "Feedback Loop": How to collect "failure cases" from employees in Phase 1 to improve the engine for Phase 2.
Permission Gates: Planning how to toggle "Advanced Features" (for employees) vs. "Simplified Features" (for clients).
Example: Employees get a "Manual DPI override" button; clients just get an "Auto-Optimize" bar.
Internal Selling Points: "This tool pays for itself in Phase 1 by cutting Adobe costs. Phase 2 is pure profit via automation."
3. Why this protects your "Finish Line"
One of the biggest reasons devs don't finish is because they try to build the "Public Version" first. Public versions require:
Perfect CSS/Design.
Complex Error Handling (for "unpredictable" users).
High-level Security Audits.
By starting with the Employee Tool, you can build a "Ugly but Powerful" version that works. Once the company sees the value, they will actually fund or support your time to polish it for the clients.
4. The "Success Criteria" for Version 1.0
In your manual, your "Definition of Done" for the first release should be:
Normalization: An employee can drop a "sideways HEIC" or "messy TIFF" and get an "upright, clean PDF."
Stapling: The employee can merge 5 different image types into 1 PDF.
Optimization: The final PDF is < 5MB without losing text clarity.

VII. The "Definition of Done" (The Kill Switch)
To prevent endless tweaking, you must define exactly when the MVP is "finished."
Feature Freeze: List the 3 features that MUST work (e.g., HEIC conversion, Rotation, Merging). Everything else (like "AI Auto-Fill") is moved to "Version 2.0."
The "Ship It" Checklist: A list of 10 tests the app must pass to be considered "v1.0."
VIII. Security & Compliance (The "Trust" Manual)
Since you're handling financial/retirement data, this is your biggest hurdle.
Data Locality Policy: Document exactly how the RAM-only processing works.
Privacy Policy Template: A draft of what you'll tell companies about how you handle their participants' PII (Personally Identifiable Information).
IX. Brand & Identity
Even if it's a utility, it needs a face.
Design System: Choose a primary color, a font, and a name.
The "Pitch Deck" for Employees: A one-pager for your coworkers to help them use it.
2. Planning the "Business Necessities"
If you want this to be a real business, you need to plan the infrastructure that sits around the code.
A. Legal Shielding
Entity Formation: Research if you should form an LLC. This separates your personal assets from the business.
The "Moonlighting" Clause: Before you write a single line, double-check your employee handbook for "Intellectual Property Assignment" rules. You want to be 100% sure you own this code.
B. The Feedback Loop
Internal Beta Group: Identify 2-3 trusted coworkers who are "non-techy." Their frustration is your most valuable data.
User Feedback Mechanism: How will people tell you it’s broken? (e.g., a simple "Submit Bug" button that sends a message to your Supabase backend).

4. Operational Requirements (The "Boring" Stuff)
You need to plan for when things go wrong.
Error States: What happens if a user uploads a 500MB video instead of a photo?
Support Plan: If you sell this to another company, how will you handle bugs while you are at your 9-to-5 job? (Answer: Automated error logging like Sentry).

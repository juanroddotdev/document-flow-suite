Internal Manual: Section 1.3 — The Legal & Employment Shield
I. The "Day Job" Audit (IP Protection)
Before writing the first line of code, you must ensure that your current employment contract doesn't have a "Global IP Grab" clause.
Review the PIIA: Look for your Proprietary Information and Inventions Agreement. Specifically, look for terms like "Assignment of Inventions."
The "Moonlighting" Rule: Ensure your contract doesn't explicitly forbid side businesses in the same industry (Financial Services/FinTech).
The Clean Room Strategy: * NEVER use a company laptop for this project.
NEVER use company software licenses (e.g., an office-provided Adobe or GitHub account).
NEVER work on this during company hours or on the company Wi-Fi.
Action Item: Keep a log of your "Development Hours" to prove they happened outside of your 9-to-5.
II. Entity Formation (The LLC)
To move from a "project" to a "business," you need to separate your personal assets from the software’s liabilities.
Formation Goal: Create a Single-Member LLC (Limited Liability Company).
Why: If a company uses your tool and claims it caused a data breach (even if it didn't), they sue the LLC, not you. It protects your house, your car, and your personal savings.
Timeline: * Phase 1 (Development): Operate as a "Project" (Sole Proprietorship).
Phase 2 (Selling): Form the LLC before signing your first contract with "Company B."
III. Licensing Strategy
Since you plan to sell this to other companies, you need to decide how they are allowed to use it.
The "Commercial License": You aren't giving them the code to own; you are giving them a License to Use.
Key Clause: "The software is provided 'As-Is' with no warranty." This is standard for small software providers to prevent being held responsible for a client's specific internal failures.
Open Source Dependencies: Since we are using pdf-lib and Lit (MIT/BSD licensed), we are safe to use them in a commercial product as long as we include their original license notices in the "About" section.
IV. The "Company B" Outreach Strategy
To avoid conflict with your current employer, your "External" outreach should follow a specific path.
Transparency (Optional): If you have a good relationship with your boss, you can ask for a "Letter of Non-Interest." This is a document where the company formally states they have no claim to your specific PDF tool.
The "White Label" Pitch: When selling to "Company B," frame it as a standalone utility you developed to solve a universal industry problem, not a tool "stolen" from your current workflow.

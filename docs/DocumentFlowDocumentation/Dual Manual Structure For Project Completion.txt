That makes perfect sense, and honestly, it’s a brilliant way to solve your "not finishing" problem. By splitting the manual into Internal and External, you are essentially creating two different types of "Accountability."
Here is how that mental model works and why it helps with completion:
1. The Internal Manual (Your "Architect's Private Journal")
This is the "messy" but honest part of the plan. It’s where you deal with your own hurdles.
The "Why am I stuck?" Log: If you hit a technical wall (e.g., TIFF conversion is slow), you document it here. It’s for your eyes only, so you don't have to sound professional.
The "Unfiltered" To-Do List: This includes the boring stuff, the "tech debt" you're knowingly creating to move fast, and your personal milestones (the rewards we discussed).
The Legal/Financial Private Notes: Your specific notes on your current employment contract, your actual profit margins, and your specific outreach strategy to "Company B."
The "Developer's Secrets": Specific API keys, environment variables, or "quick-fix" hacks that you wouldn't want a customer to see.
2. The External Manual (Your "Professional Product Face")
This is what you share with your boss, potential buyers, or the open-source community. It’s polished, clear, and confident.
The "Trust" Engine: This is where the ROI calculator, the Privacy/Security model, and the "How-To" guides live.
The Feature Roadmap: A cleaned-up version of what you're building.
Technical Integration Guides: Documentation for how their developers can take your component and drop it into their site.
The Value Proposition: Language that sells the "Agnostic Input" as a solved problem.
Why this "Completion-Proofs" the project:
Psychology: One reason people stop finishing is the pressure to make everything "look perfect" early on. By having an Internal space, you have a place to be imperfect. You can focus on the "ugly" logic while the External manual stays pretty.
Clear Boundaries: It prevents you from accidentally sharing something confidential.
Momentum: When you finish a technical feat in the "Internal" manual, you get the satisfaction of "promoting" it to the "External" manual. That act of moving information from private to public acts as a powerful psychological win.
How this looks in a tool like Starlight or VitePress:
You can actually configure these tools to have "Private Folders" that don't get published to the website, or you can keep your Internal manual in a private Obsidian or Notion vault while the External one is hosted on the web.
